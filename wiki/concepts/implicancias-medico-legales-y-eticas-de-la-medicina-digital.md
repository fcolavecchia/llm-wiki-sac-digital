# Implicancias Médico-Legales y Éticas de la Medicina Digital

## Summary

The medico-legal and ethical implications of digital medicine include professional responsibility, patient rights, privacy, data protection, informed consent, HCE integrity, prescriptions, digital signatures, remote communication, social media conduct, and AI governance. The SAC consensus emphasizes that Argentine law often has to be applied by analogy while specific digital-health regulation continues to develop.

## Detailed Explanation

The consensus anchors digital medicine in existing Argentine legal frameworks, including the law governing medical practice, patient rights, digital signature, data protection, civil and commercial rights, criminal protection of communications, and ICT communications rules. It argues that digital communication involving clinical information or indications should be treated as a medical act and documented accordingly.

Patient rights remain central: dignified treatment, privacy, confidentiality, autonomy, informed consent, and protection of sensitive health data. The HCE is treated as a public document of medical practice and should preserve integrity, authenticity, inalterability, durability, recoverability, unity, and inviolability.

## Why It Matters

Digital medicine can increase access and efficiency, but it also expands the surface for privacy breaches, undocumented medical advice, unclear responsibility, secret recordings, unsafe social media behavior, and AI-driven errors.

## Implications

- Remote clinical acts should be registered in the appropriate clinical documentation.
- Electronic or digital prescriptions must follow applicable signature and prescription rules.
- Health data are sensitive personal data and require consent, confidentiality, and secure handling.
- Professionals should be cautious with social media claims, testimonials, patient images, and publication of medical content.
- Institutions need policies for platform approval, consent, privacy, professional coverage, workload, burnout prevention, and accountability.

## Tensions or Caveats

The source says technology is advancing faster than regulation. This creates legal uncertainty around teleassistance, AI, insurance coverage, licensing, professional boundaries, and malpractice responsibility. It calls for re-education of both physicians and patients as the classic semiology and doctor-patient relationship change.
